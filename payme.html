Crafting a user-centric, intuitive experience for PayMe users

Business: PayMe
Industry: Fintech
Role: Product Designer
Timeline: 6 months

Project Summary

I designed the PayMe payment app end-to-end, based on a gap in the fintech industry I discovered during market research. The result is an engaging, human-centered design solution focused on ease of use, transparency, and customizable automation to achieve both, user and business goals.

Context

Payme is a web-based payment platform start-up supporting users with in-store & online payments, peer-to-peer money transfers, savings automation, and customizable tracking & budgeting features.

The Problem

Users do not have access to a single financial solution providing a secure, and simple way to transact with others while offering automated savings and personalized financial management tools to establish and maintain financial health.

Why it's Important

The Financial Health Pulse 2024 U.S. Trends Report found that 70% of American households are financially unhealthy, reflecting challenges in managing everyday financial responsibilities.

My user research findings were congruent with this assessment.

Objectives

Business Objectives

  • Unique value proposition
  • 1,000 users within 30 days of launch
  • 70% opt-in for novel features at 90 days

User Goals

  • Smooth transactions
  • Transparency
  • Ease of use and minimal effort
  • Customization

Design Challenges

  • Establish user trust
  • Decrease barriers for entry
  • Minimal UI
  • Emotional design for increased engagement

Approach: What's intuitive to users?

Starting with user research and concept testing, my hypothesis of the problem was validated. However, I suspected subtle gamification would prove useful to those struggling to save money by providing additional perspective in relation to their goals. This was not the case.

Regardless of the balance in a savings account, this is not the right environment for games.

Personas & User Journeys

A few personas materialized but two personas rose to prevalence and were deemed most important to the business for the MVP: the Active and Power Users.

I built user journeys to unveil additional opportunities to address user pain points for each persona.

Architecture

With a myriad of options available to me, it made the most sense to gain clarity from actual users via card sorting exercises. Technical issues with recording the first round of the study resulted in having to conduct more studies with new participants. Using AI to aggregate the results from all of the parsed studies was a huge help and got things back on track quickly.

User Flows & Lower Fidelity Prototypes

Familiar patterns for payments/money-transfers is important for the experience. Novel features need to be equally simple to use and navigate, especially when our goal is to land 1k active users in the first 30 days. Insights have already indicated that ease of use is the primary user concern.

Low-Fidelity: Savings Automation

My primary concern at this experimental stage is cognitive load, decision fatigue, and the pursuit of an intuitive experience. This includes patterns, and any interaction I might have missed, want, or need to change.

Mid-Fidelity

I've identified what appear to be the best solutions to minimize cognitive load and move on to iterate in higher fidelity. This stage is essential for me to correctly validate the mapped experience and note opportunities for secondary & tertiary micro-interactions and visuals. Now that I've established loose template standards in the earlier stage, I'm now adding more screens and drilling down on finer details.

Note: I'm relying on the magic of 3. The user should be able to setup and use any feature in 3 simple steps.

Typically:

  • Launch
  • Edit
  • Confirm

Usability

Every reasonable attempt has been made to focus on user goals while meeting the business objectives of the PayMe organization. From multiple routes to trigger savings and budgeting features to a user-assist acquisition vehicle for referrals, the organization and its product users are poised to meet those goals.

Usability Testing

I conducted another remote usability test of a simulated mobile environment on desktops/laptops with 7 participants varying in age, income, and financial wellness. My goals for this test were to observe and measure not only the usability of the product but also to gather additional data relating to how users intended to incorporate these features into their lives, and the value they felt they were being provided.

  • 100% felt the product provides value they can benefit from
  • 100% felt the product was easy to use
  • 100% were delighted by the customization of the product and felt like they had more control in the app compared to competing products

Beware the 100%s. Consensus is hardly perfection. There are plenty of chinks to work out.

Transparency & Emotional Design

33% of participants were concerned with their ability to remember their settings for automated savings at the time of purchase and worried about being surprised by the financial impacts. I flagged this as a potential accessibility issue that needed to be addressed immediately.

I also saw this as an opportunity to present users with immediate access to pause automation before individual transactions, providing even more control of their experience.

As I progressed through usability testing I also learned that although performance and task completion scores were good, I observed no evidence of an emotional connection with the product itself.

Results

The result is an intuitive, engaging platform for transacting with others while also acruing savings and categorizing transactions for budgeting insights.

The UI is clutter-free and will remain as utilitiarian as possible as the product evolves, per user insights.

Final Thoughts

Work is far from over for the PayMe app, as this is just the foundation for the MVP. I was very surprised by the current comfort level payment app users have with conventional security. I expected to dive deeper into this area from the start, but that was not the case. Moving forward, I suspect additional insights after launch will provide more clarity in regard to tightening up the budgeting feature. I'm expecting big changes for that feature to provide even better utility.

This project has been very rewarding, and I enjoyed relying on data to defend my design decisions and push forward to align with the business.
